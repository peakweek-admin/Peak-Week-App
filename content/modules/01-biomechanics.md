# Foundations of Lifting Biomechanics

Biomechanics is the language of force. The goal of this module is to teach you how force travels through the body and how tiny changes in angle, stance, or equipment can completely change which tissues do the work.

## Lesson 1: Moment Arms and Torque Curves

**Core idea:** Torque equals force times the perpendicular distance to the joint. As joint angles change, the moment arm changes, which changes torque demands.

- **Where it feels hardest** usually aligns with the longest moment arm.
- **Bar path and body position** shift the moment arm and therefore the load on each joint.
- **Torque curves** explain why some lifts feel easiest at the bottom while others feel easiest near lockout.

**Practical applications**
- Use accommodating resistance (bands/chains) to match strength curves.
- Choose variations that bias torque to the target muscle (e.g., heels-elevated squats for quads).
- Use different equipment (machines, cables) when you want a more consistent torque curve.

## Lesson 2: Lever Classes and Anthropometry

**Core idea:** Limb lengths alter leverage and joint loading.

- **Long femurs** increase hip torque in squats and require more forward torso angle.
- **Shorter arms** help pressing leverage but reduce pulling ROM.
- **Torso length** changes how much the spine must resist forward collapse.

**Practical applications**
- Adjust stance width, toe angle, and bar placement to match your limb lengths.
- Use blocks, wedges, or different shoes to restore balance.
- Don’t copy another lifter’s setup without considering structure.

## Lesson 3: Stability, Bracing, and Energy Transfer

**Core idea:** Stabilizers reduce energy leaks so prime movers can express force.

- A strong **brace** keeps the spine neutral and transfers force to the bar.
- **Scapular stability** protects the shoulder during pulls and presses.
- **Unilateral work** reveals and corrects asymmetries.

**Practical applications**
- Practice 360° breathing into the belt or abdomen before each rep.
- Add carries, planks, and slow eccentric work for stability.
- Use controlled tempo when stability is the limiting factor.

## Lesson 4: Range of Motion and Resistance Profiles

**Core idea:** Range of motion changes mechanical tension and muscle length.

- **Longer ROM** often increases hypertrophy because the muscle is stretched under load.
- **Partial ROM** can overload specific joint angles.
- **Tempo** increases time under tension without increasing load.

**Practical applications**
- Use full ROM for muscle growth phases.
- Use partials for targeted overload at sticking points.
- Combine full ROM with slow eccentrics for maximal stimulus.

## Lesson 5: Joint-Friendly Mechanics

**Core idea:** Joint stress rises when force vectors misalign with joint structure.

- **Knees** tolerate load best when tracking in line with toes.
- **Shoulders** prefer stable scapula positioning during pressing.
- **Hips** benefit from balanced rotation and stable core.

**Practical applications**
- Use wedges or heel elevation to reduce excessive forward knee travel if needed.
- Adjust bench angle and grip width to protect shoulders.
- Use activation drills when a joint feels unstable before heavy work.

## Lesson 6: Case Study — Squat vs Leg Press

**Core idea:** Same movement pattern, different stability and torque profiles.

- Squats demand more core stability and overall coordination.
- Leg presses reduce stabilizer demands, allowing higher local muscle fatigue.
- Both can be used strategically depending on goals and recovery.

**Takeaway:** Use squats for movement skill and systemic strength, leg press for targeted hypertrophy without spinal loading.

## Deep Dive: Force Vectors and Joint Stress

When you push or pull against resistance, the direction of that resistance matters just as much as the load. Force vectors that align with the joint’s natural motion generally feel smoother and safer, while vectors that fight joint alignment can create irritation over time.

- Cables let you match the line of pull to the muscle’s line of action.
- Machines often lock you into a single vector; choose ones that match your anatomy.
- Free weights give more freedom but demand higher stabilization.

## Common Mistakes

- Chasing depth at the expense of spinal position.
- Forcing a bar path that does not match your lever lengths.
- Neglecting stabilization work until pain appears.

## Practical Checklist

- Can you keep neutral spine under load?
- Do your knees track in line with toes?
- Do your shoulders stay stable and packed?
- Are your sticking points improving with targeted variation?

## Example Application: Pressing Variations

- **Flat barbell press:** high stability demand, stronger triceps contribution.
- **Incline dumbbell press:** more shoulder flexion, greater pec stretch.
- **Machine press:** reduced stability demands, easier to push to local failure.

**Action step:** Rotate one press variation per block to manage joint stress while keeping volume high.

## Field Notes: Movement Audit

Use this short audit once per month to see if your mechanics are improving.

- Video your main lift from the side and front.
- Check bar path consistency rep to rep.
- Look for knee cave, hip shift, or shoulder roll.
- Compare your setup across warm-up and work sets.

## Exercise Selection Examples

- **Quad bias:** heels-elevated squat, high-bar squat, leg press with deep knee flexion.
- **Hip bias:** low-bar squat, box squat, Romanian deadlift.
- **Shoulder-friendly press:** neutral-grip dumbbell press, machine press.

## Glossary

- **Moment arm:** perpendicular distance between joint and force line.
- **Torque:** rotational force at a joint.
- **Stability:** ability to resist unwanted motion.
- **Leverage:** mechanical advantage from limb length and joint angle.

## Case Studies: Torque Adjustments

- **Deadlift lockout weakness:** use rack pulls or banded deadlifts to shift torque toward lockout.
- **Squat off the floor weakness:** add pause squats to increase time under tension in the bottom.
- **Bench press sticking point:** use close-grip or Spoto press to overload mid-range.
