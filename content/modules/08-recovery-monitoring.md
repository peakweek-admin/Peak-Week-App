# Recovery Monitoring and Load Management

Monitoring recovery gives you a dashboard for training. Instead of guessing, you can use data to guide when to push and when to pull back.

## Lesson 1: Readiness Metrics

**Core idea:** Objective and subjective data work best together.

- HRV tracks autonomic nervous system balance.
- Resting heart rate signals systemic fatigue.
- Mood, soreness, and motivation fill in the context.

**Practical applications**
- Track metrics daily and use weekly averages.
- Don’t overreact to single-day dips.
- Use a short readiness survey to capture subjective data.

## Lesson 2: Load Management Models

**Core idea:** Acute-to-chronic workload balances progress with recovery.

- Avoid sudden spikes in volume or intensity.
- Build gradual ramps across mesocycles.
- Track weekly sets per muscle group.

**Practical applications**
- Increase volume 5–10% per week during accumulation.
- Use lighter weeks after travel or high stress.
- Adjust intensity before volume when recovery drops.

## Lesson 3: Deload Strategy and Tapering

**Core idea:** Planned reductions restore performance.

- Cut volume 30–50% while keeping technique sharp.
- Keep some intensity to avoid losing neural adaptations.
- Use tapers before performance peaks.

**Practical applications**
- Schedule deloads every 4–8 weeks.
- Use tapering before testing maxes.
- Combine deloads with diet maintenance weeks.

## Lesson 4: Recovery Interventions

**Core idea:** Interventions are multipliers, not replacements.

- Mobility and light cardio support circulation.
- Hydration and electrolytes support performance.
- Massage and contrast therapy are secondary tools.

**Practical applications**
- Use active recovery on rest days.
- Prioritize sleep before adding advanced tools.
- Track whether interventions actually improve readiness.

## Lesson 5: Building Your Recovery Dashboard

**Core idea:** A simple system beats a complex one you won’t use.

- Track sleep hours, stress level, and training performance.
- Use color coding to flag low readiness days.
- Review weekly trends to guide planning.

**Takeaway:** Recovery monitoring keeps progress steady and reduces injury risk.

## Deep Dive: Interpreting HRV Trends

HRV is best used as a trend, not a single-day verdict. A short-term dip might reflect poor sleep, while a multi-day decline signals systemic fatigue.

- High HRV often indicates good recovery.
- Low HRV over multiple days suggests volume reduction.
- Pair HRV with subjective data for context.

## Common Mistakes

- Training hard despite multi-day fatigue trends.
- Overreacting to a single low HRV score.
- Ignoring subjective readiness signals.

## Practical Checklist

- Are your metrics stable week to week?
- Do low HRV days align with stress or sleep loss?
- Are you adjusting training based on trends?

## Example Application: Readiness-Based Training

- Green days: follow planned progression.
- Yellow days: reduce volume by 20–30%.
- Red days: focus on technique or recovery.

**Action step:** Use a simple traffic-light system to guide daily decisions.

## Field Notes: Weekly Review

A short weekly review improves decision-making.

- Summarize your average HRV and sleep hours.
- Rate overall training quality 1–5.
- Note any joints that feel irritated.

## Exercise Selection Examples

- **High fatigue weeks:** reduce barbell volume, use machines.
- **Low fatigue weeks:** push progression in compounds.
- **Mixed weeks:** keep volume steady, add light accessory work.

## Glossary

- **Acute load:** recent training stress.
- **Chronic load:** long-term training base.
- **Readiness:** overall capacity to handle training stress.

## Self-Test Prompts

- Are you making changes based on trends, not one-day data?
- Are your deloads restoring energy and performance?
- Do subjective and objective data agree?

## Case Studies: Readiness Decisions

- **HRV drop for 3 days:** reduce volume and add sleep focus.
- **Subjective fatigue but normal HRV:** keep intensity, reduce volume.
- **Performance drop:** schedule deload earlier.
